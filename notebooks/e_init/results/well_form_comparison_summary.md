# Functional-form comparison for the per-sentence potential well

_Companion experiment targeted at paper **v2** (does not affect v1).
Generated by `notebooks/e_init/well_functional_form_comparison.py`._

**Model:** GPT-2 (12 layers, d=768) &nbsp;&nbsp; **Corpus:** 50 sentences,
1,413 per-token (sentence, position) trajectories, 1,363 with NTP-loss
targets &nbsp;&nbsp; **Centering:** per-sentence per-layer.

## 1. Question

Section 14.6 of the paper flags _functional-form discrimination_ (Outcome
C) as the natural next step when the Gaussian well under-performs. Here
we ask concretely: **does any member of the bounded-attractive class,
other than Gaussian, fit the empirical $(\lVertx\rVert, \text{NTP loss})$
scatter better, and -- more importantly -- does using it rescue the
damped second-order integrator?**

Seven functional forms are tested per layer (all add a constant offset
$c$):

| form | $V(r)$ | notes |
|---|---|---|
| harmonic | $a r^{2}$ | simplest, unbounded |
| gaussian | $a(1-e^{-br^{2}})$ | reference form from v1 |
| morse | $a(1-e^{-br})^{2}$ | uses $r$, not $r^{2}$ |
| rational (Lorentzian) | $abr^{2}/(1+br^{2})$ | saturates polynomially |
| log-saturation | $a\log(1+br^{2})$ | slow saturation |
| Weibull / stretched-exp | $a(1-e^{-br^{\alpha}})$ | generalises Gaussian via $\alpha$ |
| power law | $a r^{p}$ | unbounded with free exponent |

**Findings.**

- Across all middle layers (2-11) every non-trivial form reaches
  $R^{2}\approx 0.107\text{-}0.108$; the differences are in the fourth
  decimal. The power law wins the raw comparison at most layers but
  only by $\lesssim 0.0005$.
- Gaussian is the only form that gets $R^{2}=0.000$ at layers 1 and
  12; harmonic, morse, rational, logsat, and power all do markedly
  better there ($R^{2}\approx 0.09$ at layer 1). This matches the
  paper's observation that the Gaussian fit is especially bad at the
  embedding and final layers.
- Weibull (stretched exponential) does _not_ help; the fitter lets
  $\alpha\to 2$, collapsing to Gaussian, and when Gaussian fits poorly
  it tends to degrade.

**This is the most important table in the document.** On binned medians
the deterministic trend is clearly recovered: $R^{2}\approx 0.82\text{-}0.90$
at layers 3-7 across essentially all forms. Two things follow:

1. There really is a strong deterministic relationship $\|x\|\mapsto
   \langle \text{NTP loss}\rangle$ in the middle layers; the raw scatter
   masks it. (This is itself a paper-worthy observation -- it is the
   cleanest evidence so far that a well-defined potential is at work.)
2. All seven functional forms capture this trend _almost identically_.
   The power law edges the rest by $\lesssim 0.002$, and harmonic /
   gaussian / morse / rational / logsat are indistinguishable at four
   decimals. The common binned shape is, empirically, nearly quadratic:
   the best-fit power-law exponent is $p\approx 2.3$-$2.5$ at middle
   layers, and the fitted quadratic coefficient at, e.g., layer 4 is
   $a\approx 10^{-6}$ -- exactly what one gets from the Gaussian in the
   small-$br^{2}$ limit.

## 4. Integrator residuals with the new well forms

For each form we rebuild the damped second-order Euler-Lagrange integrator
with that form's analytic gradient, sweep $\gamma$, and measure the median
layer-$L$ residual under per-sentence centering. We also run an
AIC-selected mixed map (different form per layer).

Static-null baseline median layer-$L$ residual = **0.1773**.

**All eight configurations return identical residuals to four decimal
places.** This is not a coding bug -- the mechanism is quantitative:

At a typical per-sentence-centered radius $r\approx 300$ (middle layers),
the force-per-unit-displacement $k(r) := V'(r)/r$ is, for each form:

| form | $k(r=300)$ at layer 4 |
|---|---:|
| harmonic | $1.51\times 10^{-6}$ |
| gaussian | $1.67\times 10^{-6}$ |
| morse | $1.61\times 10^{-6}$ |
| rational | $1.79\times 10^{-6}$ |
| log-saturation | $1.69\times 10^{-6}$ |
| Weibull | $3.07\times 10^{-6}$ |
| power | $0.66\times 10^{-6}$ |

Every form is in the same narrow band of $\sim 10^{-6}$. The absolute
scale is what matters: a force $\sim 10^{-6}\cdot\lVertx\rVert$ accumulated over
12 layers with damping $\gamma\gtrsim 1$ produces a displacement far
below the layer-to-layer motion in GPT-2, so the integrator outputs
collapse onto each other -- and onto the static null.

The force scale is set by the fitted $a$ (well depth $\approx 8$ nats)
divided by the _squared_ data scale ($\lVertx\rVert^{2}\approx 10^{5}$). This
ratio is $\sim 10^{-4}$ per unit $r^{2}$, and the $r$-derivative picks
up another factor of $\sim 10^{-2}\lVertx\rVert$, giving the $\sim 10^{-6}$
number. It is a property of the _data geometry_, not of the functional
choice.

## 5. Answer to the user's question

> "Can you fit a different potential well, not Gaussian, which will fit
> the experiment results?"

**No form in the bounded-attractive / power class tested here
produces a meaningfully different integrator outcome.** Specifically:

- **On fit quality alone**, the power law, harmonic, and Morse forms
  each edge out the Gaussian by $\sim 0.001$ in raw $R^{2}$ and
  $\sim 0.002$-$0.07$ in binned-median $R^{2}$. The only visible
  improvement is at layers 1 and 12, where every non-Gaussian form
  escapes the $R^{2}=0$ trap the Gaussian falls into. That is worth
  noting in v2.
- **On integrator behaviour**, all forms give median layer-$L$ residual
  $0.1768$ at $\gamma^{*}=5.0$, identical to the Gaussian and essentially
  identical to the static null ($0.1773$). The per-token fraction-beats-null
  is the same 68.4% we saw for Gaussian.
- **The limiting factor is the data geometry**, not the potential's
  shape. The fitted well depth ($a\approx 8$ nats) divided by the data's
  typical radius squared ($\sim 10^{5}$) gives an effective per-layer
  force too weak to redirect trajectories at the scale the integrator
  operates on. No choice of $V(r)$ alone can fix this.

## 6. What would actually move the residual

The functional-form sweep narrows down the list of plausible follow-ups
for v2 considerably:

1. **Change the geometry, not the form.** Per-phrase centering
   (Prediction P4 in §10.3) or PCA projection (§14.5) reduce the
   effective $\lVertx\rVert$ scale by an order of magnitude or more, and
   therefore raise the effective force by the same factor. This is the
   lever most likely to produce a non-trivial residual drop.
2. **Let $a$ depend on position within the phrase.** The binned-median
   $R^{2}\approx 0.90$ shows there is a strong radial signal, but the
   pooled fit is shallow because the phrase-mean centroid mixes basins
   of different depths. Per-phrase $(a,b)$ fits should yield larger
   $a$ and therefore stronger forces.
3. **Drop $\lVertx\rVert$ as the sole state variable.** The residual bound
   may reflect that $V$ depends on a phrase-embedding or attention
   feature that is not captured by distance-from-centroid alone.

The functional-form discrimination that §14.6 Outcome C referred to
_has_ been attempted here in the full bounded-attractive + power class;
the negative result is a useful constraint on what the v2 rewrite of
§14 should say. In particular, §14.6 can now state that the failure of
E-init is structural in the radial coordinate, not in the Gaussian-vs.-
Morse dimension.
