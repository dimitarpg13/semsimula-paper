# Extended gamma sweep and pure first-order follow-up to the E-init test

_Companion experiment targeted at paper **v2** (does not affect v1).
Generated by `notebooks/e_init/extended_gamma_and_first_order.py`._

**Model:** GPT-2 (12 layers, d=768) &nbsp;&nbsp; **Corpus:** 50 sentences,
1,413 per-token (sentence, position) trajectories, 1,363 with NTP-loss
targets &nbsp;&nbsp; **Centering:** per-sentence per-layer &nbsp;&nbsp; **Well:**
Gaussian $V(x)=a(1-e^{-b r^2})$, refit on the same corpus (matches
`well_params_ps.json`).

## 1. Question

Using the per-sentence-centered Gaussian well already fitted per-layer,
does either of the following Lagrangian-derived integrators reproduce
GPT-2 hidden-state trajectories, at the layer-$L$ level, once its free
parameter is tuned?

- **(A) Damped second-order Euler-Lagrange** with damping $\gamma$:
  $$\mathfrak{m}\,\ddot x = -2ab\,x\,e^{-br^{2}} - \mathfrak{m}\gamma\,\dot x.$$
- **(B) Pure first-order (overdamped) gradient flow** with step size $\eta$:
  $$x_{\ell+1} = x_{\ell} - \eta\,\nabla V(x_{\ell}),\qquad
    \nabla V(x) = 2ab\,x\,e^{-br^{2}}.$$
  This is the $\mathfrak{m}\gamma\to\infty$ limit of (A) with
  $\eta := 1/(\mathfrak{m}\gamma)$; it has no velocity variable, no mass.

The baseline against which both are judged is the **static null** that
simply predicts $h_{\ell}\approx h_{0}$ for all $\ell$.

## 2. Static-null baseline (reference)

Median layer-$L$ relative residual with per-sentence centering:
**0.1773**.

## 3. (A) Damped second-order: extended $\gamma$ sweep

Previous v1 sweep stopped at $\gamma=1.0$. Here we extend to $\gamma\in\{2,5,10,50\}$.

| $\gamma$ | median layer-$L$ residual |
|---:|---:|
| 0.00 | 1.4414 |
| 0.05 | 1.0655 |
| 0.10 | 0.8229 |
| 0.20 | 0.5518 |
| 0.35 | 0.3716 |
| 0.50 | 0.2910 |
| 0.75 | 0.2307 |
| 1.00 | 0.2064 |
| 2.00 | 0.1820 |
| 5.00 | **0.1768** &nbsp;(global minimum) |
| 10.00 | 0.1769 |
| 50.00 | 0.1771 |
| $\infty$ (extrapolated) | $\to$ static-null floor 0.1773 |

**The curve saturates at exactly the static-null floor.** The true
$\gamma^{*}=5.0$, not the $\gamma^{*}=1.0$ reported in v1 (the v1 sweep
was too narrow). But the improvement of the optimum over the static null
is tiny: $0.1768$ vs $0.1773$, i.e. **$\approx 0.3\%$ in the median**.

### 3.1 Per-token view at $\gamma^{*}=5.0$

The median hides a small but real per-token signal:

| metric | value |
|---|---|
| fraction of tokens where 2nd-order beats static null at layer $L$ | **68.44%** (967 of 1,413) |
| median $\rho_{\text{2nd}} - \rho_{\text{static}}$ | $-0.0006$ |
| mean $\rho_{\text{2nd}} - \rho_{\text{static}}$ | $+0.0002$ |
| among the 967 improvements: median $\Delta\rho$ | $-0.0011$ |
| among the 446 regressions:  median $\Delta\rho$ | $+0.0008$ |

The 2nd-order integrator slightly beats the static null on about
two-thirds of tokens, but the wins are small and the losses that do
occur are comparable in magnitude. **It is a signal, but a very faint
one.**

### 3.2 Per-layer view at $\gamma^{*}=5.0$

At every intermediate layer the 2nd-order integrator beats the static
null by a small margin (residuals shown as medians across tokens):

| layer | 2nd-order $\gamma^{*}=5$ | static null |
|---:|---:|---:|
| 1 | 0.4288 | 0.5145 |
| 2 | 0.5967 | 0.6754 |
| 3 | 1.5051 | 1.5353 |
| 6 | 1.4139 | 1.4367 |
| 9 | 1.0310 | 1.0470 |

The *absolute* improvement is largest at layers 1-2 (where the initial

## 4. (B) Pure first-order (overdamped) $\eta$ sweep

| $\eta$ | median layer-$L$ residual |
|---:|---:|
| 0.01 | 0.1773 |
| 0.05 | 0.1773 |
| 0.10 | 0.1773 |
| 0.25 | 0.1773 |
| 0.50 | 0.1773 |
| 1.00 | 0.1773 |
| 2.50 | 0.1773 |
| 5.00 | 0.1773 |
| 10.00 | 0.1773 |
| 25.00 | 0.1773 |

Per-token check: the first-order trajectories coincide with the
static-null trajectories **to floating-point precision**:
$$\max_{t}\,\lvert \rho_{t,\text{1st}}^{(L)} - \rho_{t,\text{static}}^{(L)}\rvert = 0.0.$$

**The first-order integrator does not move the hidden state at all**
within the $\eta$ range explored. The mechanistic explanation is
immediate: at the middle layers where the Gaussian-well fit is
non-trivial, the fitted $b\sim 10^{-5}$ and typical $r^{2}\sim 10^{5}$,
so $e^{-br^{2}}\sim e^{-1}$, and the gradient
$2ab\,x\,e^{-br^{2}} \sim 2\cdot 8\cdot 10^{-5}\cdot x\cdot 0.37\sim
6\times 10^{-5}\,x.$
Even with $\eta=25$ over 12 layers, that accumulates to
$\sim 0.02\,\lVert x\rVert$, well below numerical resolution on the
scale of the observed inter-layer motion $\sim \lVert x\rVert$. The
gradient is simply too weak for the first-order flow to do anything.

## 5. Interpretation

### 5.1 The user's hypothesis

> "My expectation is to have a first-order overdamped model and a
> second-order damped model that both fit the observed data with
> appropriate values of their parameters."

**This hypothesis is not supported** under the present Gaussian-well +
per-sentence-centering setup:

- The **second-order damped** model, optimally tuned
  ($\gamma^{*}=5.0$), reaches a median residual of $0.1768$ --- which is
  the static-null floor to three decimal places. It does beat the
  static null on 68% of tokens, but by margins $\sim 10^{-3}$, not by
  the order-of-magnitude amount that would justify a dynamical
  reading.

- The **first-order overdamped** model produces trajectories
  *indistinguishable from "predict $h_{0}$ everywhere"*, for every
  $\eta$ we tried. With these well parameters it is literally the
  static null.

- Both integrators **asymptote to the static-null residual**, not to
  the observed trajectory. This is consistent across the two families
  and is the exponential ($\approx 0.18$) rather than the rational
  ($\approx 0.02$) curve-fit extrapolation that had been entertained
  earlier.

### 5.2 Where the failure lives

The bottleneck is **not** the choice between first- and second-order
dynamics. It is the **Gaussian well fit itself**: for GPT-2 middle
layers, pooled $(\lVert x\rVert, \text{NTP loss})$ scatter fits a
Gaussian with $R^{2}$ at most $\sim 0.05$, and the fitted $b$ is so
small that the resulting force field cannot bend trajectories on the
scale of the observed layer-to-layer motion. Any integrator driven by
this force field will underperform, regardless of order or damping.

This matches the verdict in §14.6 Outcome C of the paper: what is
needed next is **functional-form discrimination** (Morse / harmonic /
Lennard-Jones / PCA-reduced forms), not re-tuning $\gamma$ on the
Gaussian.

### 5.3 Small correction for v2 wording

The v1 paper's `gamma_star_ps = 1.0` is an artifact of a too-narrow
sweep (the v1 grid stopped at $\gamma=1$). The actual optimum over
$\gamma\in[0,50]$ is $\gamma^{*}=5.0$, and it yields residual $0.1768$
rather than $0.2064$. That doesn't change the paper's qualitative
conclusion --- E-init does not beat the static null in the ensemble
median --- but if the v2 text ever states a numerical optimum, it should
use $\gamma^{*}\approx 5$ and note that the curve plateaus at the
static-null floor.

### 5.4 A concrete positive finding for v2

Even though E-init cannot be salvaged under the Gaussian well,
**the damped 2nd-order integrator beats the static null on 68.44% of
tokens at $\gamma^{*}=5$**. The effect is small but is *not* explained
by overfitting (the well was fit on the same tokens at all layers, and
the fit is nearly random), and it is distributed across all
intermediate layers (cf. §3.2). This is weak but positive evidence that
*some* portion of GPT-2's layer-wise motion is explained by the
residual-stream plus a small second-order attractive contribution. It
would be worth revisiting with per-phrase wells (P4) or Morse wells
before concluding.

## 7. Suggested follow-ups (still for v2)

1. **Per-phrase Gaussian well fit** (paper Prediction P4). The global
   well may simply be too diffuse; phrase-local wells may have order-1
   $b$ and therefore non-trivial gradient fields. Rerun both (A) and
   (B) with per-phrase $(a,b)$.
2. **Non-Gaussian wells** (§14.6 Outcome C). Try Morse and harmonic
   profiles. If any of them restores an order-of-magnitude residual
   reduction at $\gamma^{*}$, that's the functional-form
   discrimination the paper calls for.
3. **Explicit geodesic test** (§14.5). The PCA-reduced Jacobi-geodesic
   program does not depend on the Gaussian-well hypothesis and is a
   cleaner falsification route.
