# Helmholtz-augmented Euler–Lagrange: does a linear solenoidal term fit the trajectories?

Model: **gpt2**.  TRAIN: 40 sentences / 1 127 tokens.  TEST: 10 sentences / 286 tokens
(balanced 2-per-domain split over `mathematics`, `narrative`, `scientific`,
`code_description`, `conversational`).  PCA subspace: **k = 16 per layer**.
Ridge regulariser: **1 × 10⁻³**.

## 1. Question

The previous functional-form sweep ([`well_form_comparison_summary.md`][1])
showed that **no** scalar potential $V(x)$ — Gaussian, harmonic, Morse,
rational, log-saturation, Weibull, or power law — reduces the layer-$L$
residual below the static-null floor.  By the Helmholtz decomposition any
sufficiently regular force field can be written

$$\vec F(\vec x)\;=\;-\nabla V(\vec x)\;+\;\vec F_{\mathrm s}(\vec x),\qquad \nabla\cdot\vec F_{\mathrm s}=0.$$

This experiment asks whether *adding* a linear solenoidal term

$$\vec F_{\mathrm s}(\vec x)\;=\;V_\ell\,\Omega_\ell\,V_\ell^{\top}\vec x,\qquad \Omega_\ell=-\Omega_\ell^{\top}\in\mathbb R^{k\times k},$$

(where $V_\ell\in\mathbb R^{d\times k}$ is the per-layer top-$k$ PCA basis of
**training** hidden states) lets the damped second-order integrator

$$\mathfrak m\,\ddot x = -\nabla V(x)+V_\ell\Omega_\ell V_\ell^{\top}x-\mathfrak m\,\gamma\,\dot x$$

actually fit the hidden-state trajectories on held-out sentences.  As an
**upper bound** that brackets the skew contribution, we also fit the
unconstrained linear operator $M_\ell\in\mathbb R^{k\times k}$ (no skew
constraint) — its symmetric part cannot be generated by any scalar
potential with a fixed Hessian (it is *not* guaranteed to be a Hessian)
so this is strictly richer than either Helmholtz component alone.

Static-null reference (predict $h_0$ at every layer):
- TRAIN median layer-$L$ residual = **0.1771**
- TEST  median layer-$L$ residual = **0.1796**

and by $M$ at fit time.  They are not integrated-trajectory residuals.

### 2.2 Best-$\gamma$ slice (TEST set)

$\gamma^{*}=5.0$ minimises the plain-gaussian integrator on both folds.

| configuration | TEST median layer-$L$ | Δ vs. static null |
|---|--:|--:|
| gaussian only (no curl) | 0.1774 | **−0.0022** |
| + skew $\Omega$ (Helmholtz solenoidal) | 0.1882 | +0.0086 |
| + unconstrained linear $M$ | 0.2890 | +0.1094 |

Both augmentations make the fit **worse** on held-out data.  The gap
between +skew and +full on TEST is 0.1008 — the symmetric-linear
sub-component of $M$ therefore contributes *more* error on held-out
data than the skew sub-component, which is consistent with the severe
train-test generalisation loss visible in the TRAIN +full / TEST +full
columns above.

## 3. Interpretation

### 3.1 The symmetric part of per-layer dynamics is real; the skew part is not

The full linear operator $M$ explains **78 %** of PCA-subspace residual
variance at fit time, averaged across layers — a genuinely strong linear
approximation of what the transformer block does in one layer.  The
skew projection $\Omega=\frac12(M-M^{\top})$ of the same fitted operator
has **fit $R^2\approx-13$ to $-21$**: applying $\Omega$ alone to $x$
produces a vector with larger magnitude than the true layer-wise force
residual and is essentially orthogonal to it.

Translation: per-layer hidden-state dynamics are dominated by a
**symmetric-linear stretching** component, not by a rotational one.
There is a well-defined locally-linear description of each block; it is
just almost entirely the non-rotational part.

### 3.2 This breaks both Helmholtz halves simultaneously

This is important for the theoretical narrative: the decomposition

$$F = \underbrace{-\nabla V}_{\text{conservative}} + \underbrace{F_{\mathrm s}}_{\text{solenoidal}}$$

is *exact* in an ideal sense, but both halves are represented very
poorly by the natural physical parameterisations we have available.

- The conservative half assumes $F$ is a gradient.  We showed in
  [`well_form_comparison_summary.md`][1] that seven different
  functional forms for $V(r)$, all aligned with the $(|x|,\text{NTP})$
  scatter, still produce force magnitudes $\sim 10^{-6}$ and integrator
  residuals that tie the static null.
- The solenoidal half, in its simplest $F_{\mathrm s}=\Omega x$ form,
  is essentially orthogonal to the observed per-layer residual in the
  top-16 PCA subspace.

The implication is that the best-fitting linear Jacobian of each
transformer block is approximately symmetric, but **not** Hessian-of-a-
scalar: it is a symmetric non-integrable field that neither $\nabla V$
nor $V\Omega V^{\top}x$ captures.  This is exactly what one expects
from a composition of (LayerNorm ∘ Attention ∘ MLP) — attention
softmax + LayerNorm both contribute state-dependent anisotropic
rescaling, which linearises locally to a symmetric-non-Hessian operator.

### 3.3 Why the integrator blows up even when the per-layer linear fit is good

The full-$M$ configuration has $R^2\approx 0.78$ per layer yet produces
integrated TEST residual 0.289, nearly double the static null.  This is
the classic horizon problem of local-linear approximation:

- A linear operator $M_\ell$ fits the observed step $x_{\ell+1}-x_\ell$
  given $x_\ell$, for $x_\ell$ drawn from the training distribution at
  layer $\ell$.
- The integrator then *applies* $M_\ell$ to $\hat x_\ell$, where
  $\hat x_\ell$ is the integrator's prediction, which drifts off the
  training manifold as errors compound.
- The **nonlinearities in each block** — especially LayerNorm's per-token
  rescaling and softmax saturation — mean the true Jacobian of the block
  is not a constant operator.  The integrator sees amplified extrapolation
  error over 12 layers.

### 3.4 A positive corollary for v2

Even though this experiment fails to beat the static null, its fit-quality
column carries a concrete, publishable number:

> **A constant linear per-layer operator in the top-16 PCA subspace
> captures ~78 % of the layer-wise force residual for GPT-2**.

That is a legitimate local characterisation of the block's effective
dynamics.  It also places an empirical ceiling on what *any* linear
Lagrangian theory — conservative or solenoidal, symmetric or skew —
can achieve for layer-wise hidden-state trajectories without going
nonlinear or position-dependent.
