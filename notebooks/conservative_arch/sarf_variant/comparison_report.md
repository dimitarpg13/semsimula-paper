## 4. Interpretation

### 4.1 LM quality: SARF wins cleanly

The two variants share the exact same parameter count, optimiser,
learning-rate schedule, dataset, batch size, block size, and step
budget -- the only difference is whether `xi_t^{(ell)}` is the
fixed layer-0 cumulative mean (baseline) or is recomputed from
the current hidden states at every integration step (SARF).

SARF reaches noticeably lower validation cross-entropy (5.26 vs
5.66 at the end of training), a ~33% reduction in perplexity,
and trains in essentially the same wall-clock time.  The extra
cumulative sum is O(T*d) per layer versus the O(T*d*v_hidden)
already paid by the V-MLP, so the asymptotic efficiency claim
from Appendix B is unchanged -- SARF is neither cheaper nor
more expensive in any regime we tested.

### 4.2 Shared-$V_\psi$ fit (depth axis): softer separator

The strict shared-potential fit degrades modestly with SARF:
overall TEST $R^2$ drops from +0.79 (baseline) to +0.71 (SARF),
and per-layer TEST $R^2$ medians shift from ~+0.82 to ~+0.72.
This is expected: once `xi` depends on the full sequence
`{h_s^{(ell)}}_{s<=t}`, the effective pointwise force on
`h_t^{(ell)}` is no longer the gradient of a scalar of
`h_t^{(ell)}` alone -- it is the `h_t`-component of the
gradient of a *sequence-level* functional.  A pointwise shared
scalar $V_\psi(h)$ can only recover part of that structure.

Two observations:

1.  SARF's per-layer profile is *smoother* than baseline's.
    Baseline SPLM exhibited a layer-4 TEST dip to +0.28;
    under SARF the dip disappears and layer 4 reaches +0.81.
    The extra cross-token information seems to regularise the
    learned dynamics so that no single depth cut is
    pathological.
2.  SARF remains much closer to baseline SPLM than to GPT-2
    in absolute terms.  In the three-way comparison
    (SPLM / matched-GPT / pretrained GPT-2, §14.4) the
    GPT-2 track collapses to ~+0.45 with mid-layer dips into
    single-digit R^2.  SARF's +0.71 overall, with no layer
    below +0.48, keeps the shared-potential *separator*
    intact.

### 4.3 Token-direction fit: same pattern, same magnitude

The token-axis diagnostic tells the same story: baseline
TEST $R^2 \approx +0.52$, SARF $\approx +0.40$.  The loss is
similar in absolute magnitude to the depth-axis drop (~0.10)
and is spread uniformly across layers, consistent with a
soft sequence-level coupling rather than a layer-specific
failure.

### 4.4 Are we sacrificing efficiency for accuracy?

No, we are sacrificing *structural purity* for LM quality.
Concretely:

- **Compute efficiency is unchanged.**  Wall-clock, FLOPs,
  and parameters are all within noise of each other.
  Autoregressive decoding still has no KV cache, and the
  extra cumulative-mean sum is O(T*d) per layer -- vanishing
  next to V-MLP cost.
- **LM accuracy (next-token cross-entropy) is improved.**
  Val perplexity drops by ~33% at identical compute budget.
- **Dynamical purity is softly reduced.**  The shared-scalar
  fit drops by ~0.08--0.11 in $R^2$ along both time axes.
  SARF's dynamics are no longer *pointwise* conservative;
  they are the gradient of a sequence-level functional that
  a pointwise $V_\psi$ can only approximate.

The prescriptive narrative of paper_v2 therefore survives
intact, with a refined claim.  The shared-potential
separator (SPLM-like ~+0.7--0.9 vs GPT-2 ~+0.45 with
mid-layer dips) is robust to the xi re-pooling.  What xi
re-pooling buys is exactly the kind of non-local coupling
that the framework's SARF (time-dependent reinforcement
field) motivates in the first place -- and empirically it
translates into meaningfully better LM quality, at no
additional compute.

Open question for future work: can we quantify the
sequence-level functional explicitly, so that the
structural fit comes back up to baseline SPLM levels
*and* we keep the LM gain?
