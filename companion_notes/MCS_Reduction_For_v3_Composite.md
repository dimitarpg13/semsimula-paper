# MCS Reduction for the v0+v1.5+v2+v3 Composite Simulator

**A formal reduction to Linear Context-Free Rewriting Systems**

Companion to `docs/Advancing_The_Dynamic_Simulation_Model.md` and `docs/Expressivity_Bounds_For_v0_Simulator.md`.

This note proves the central upper-bound claim of the dynamic-simulation framework: that the composite v0+v1.5+v2+v3 simulator, under explicit boundedness assumptions on its operator algebra, generates exactly the **mildly context-sensitive** (MCS) class of formal languages — equivalently, the class of LCFRS / MCFG languages of bounded fan-out and bounded rank. The reduction is constructive in both directions and verifies the four classical MCS criteria of Joshi (1985).

The expressivity-bounds note `docs/Expressivity_Bounds_For_v0_Simulator.md` formalises the *lower* boundary (v0 alone is regular). The present note formalises the *upper* boundary (the full composite is exactly MCS) and the design choices that fix it there. Together the two notes pin the framework on both sides of the Chomsky hierarchy.

---

## 1. Statement of the claim

**Theorem (sketch).** Let $\mathcal{S}$ denote the composite v0+v1.5+v2+v3 dynamic-simulation system, equipped with

- *a v2 Fock-space architecture in which each particle of type $A$ carries a state vector $x^A \in \mathbb{R}^{k_A \cdot d}$, with $k_A$ a fixed positive integer (the particle's **fan-out**) and $d$ a global state-dimension constant;*
- *a v3 operator algebra realised as the action of a finite-dimensional Lie group $G$ on $\bigoplus_A \mathbb{R}^{k_A \cdot d}$ with finite rank, with operator-arity bounded by a constant $r_{\max}$;*
- *the v0 EOM (`docs/Semantic_Simulator_EOM.md`) and the v1.5 salient-decay rule of §5.1 of `docs/Advancing_The_Dynamic_Simulation_Model.md`.*

*Under these assumptions, the language family $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{S})$ generated by $\mathcal{S}$ — defined as the set of finite terminal-yield strings producible by some sequence of v2-creation and v3-operator events emitting symbols at termination — is exactly*

$$
\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{S}) = \mathrm{MCS} = \mathcal{L}(\text{LCFRS of bounded fan-out and rank}) \qquad(1.1)
$$

*as classes of formal languages.*

The claim is *both directions*: every MCS language can be generated by a composite simulator with appropriate $G$ and $\{k_A\}$, and every language generated by a composite simulator with bounded $\{k_A\}$ and $r_{\max}$ is MCS. The two directions correspond to the two halves of §3 below.

A tightening of any of the three boundedness assumptions changes the class:

- **If $\{k_A\}$ is unbounded** (allowing arbitrary fan-out), $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{S})$ rises to the recursively enumerable class via a register-machine encoding.
- **If $r_{\max}$ is unbounded** (allowing arbitrary rank LCFRS-like rules), the parsing complexity blows up and the constant-growth property fails; the class again exceeds MCS.
- **If $G$ is replaced by a Lie groupoid that admits arbitrary word-equation composition**, the simulator can decide the word problem on free groups and is Turing-complete.

The three assumptions are therefore necessary and sufficient for the MCS reach. This is what we mean by *the framework lands at MCS by design choice*: each of the three is a deliberate restriction with linguistic motivation.

## 2. LCFRS / MCFG primer

This section is self-contained for readers unfamiliar with the formalism. Standard references: Vijay-Shanker, Weir & Joshi (1987), Seki et al. (1991), and Kallmeyer (2010, Chapter 4).

A **linear context-free rewriting system** (LCFRS) is a tuple $(N, T, P, S)$ with:

- $N$ a finite set of non-terminals, each $A \in N$ assigned a fixed **fan-out** $\mathrm{fo}(A) = k \in \mathbb{N}$.
- $T$ a finite set of terminals.
- $P$ a finite set of rules of the form

$$
A(\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_k) \to f[B_1(\vec y_1), \ldots, B_r(\vec y_r)] \qquad(2.1)
$$

where each $B_j$ has fan-out $k_j$, the variables $\vec y_j = (y_{j,1}, \ldots, y_{j,k_j})$ are pairwise disjoint, and each $\alpha_i$ is a string built from terminals and the $y_{j,k}$ variables by concatenation, subject to the **linearity constraint**: each variable $y_{j,k}$ appears exactly once on the left-hand side.

- $S \in N$ is the start symbol with $\mathrm{fo}(S) = 1$.

The **rank** of a rule is its $r$ (the number of right-hand non-terminals). The **maximum fan-out** of the LCFRS is $\max_A \mathrm{fo}(A)$. An LCFRS is **bounded** if both fan-out and rank are bounded by a global constant.

A derivation produces a tuple of strings via tree composition; the start symbol's single string is the **yield**, and the yield-set is the language $\mathcal{L}$.

**Theorem (Vijay-Shanker, Weir & Joshi 1987; Seki et al. 1991).** *The class of bounded LCFRS languages is the class of mildly context-sensitive languages.* It is equivalent in expressive power to:

- multiple context-free grammars (MCFGs);
- Tree-Adjoining Grammars (TAGs) at fan-out 2, rank 2;
- Combinatory Categorial Grammars (CCGs) at the same complexity tier;
- head grammars.

The class admits the four characterising properties of Joshi (1985):

1. **Constant growth.** The set of derivation lengths is semilinear; every derivation extends the yield by a bounded number of terminals.
2. **Polynomial parsing.** LCFRS of fan-out $k$ and rank $r$ is parsable in time $O(n^{(r+1)k})$ where $n$ is input length. For TAG / fan-out-2 / rank-2 LCFRS this is $O(n^6)$.
3. **Beyond context-free.** The class properly includes languages such as $\{a^n b^n c^n\}$, $\{ww : w \in \Sigma^*\}$, and Swiss-German cross-serial dependencies — all of which are not context-free.
4. **Below context-sensitive.** The class is strictly below the full context-sensitive class of LBA-recognisable languages.

These four properties are the target of the reduction.

## 3. The reduction

We prove the equivalence in (1.1) by two embeddings: every bounded LCFRS is a composite simulator (§3.1, the *completeness* direction), and every composite simulator with the assumed boundedness is a bounded LCFRS (§3.2, the *soundness* direction).

### 3.1 Every bounded LCFRS is a composite simulator (completeness)

Given a bounded LCFRS $\mathcal{G} = (N, T, P, S)$ with maximum fan-out $K = \max_A \mathrm{fo}(A)$ and maximum rank $R = \max_p \mathrm{rank}(p)$, we construct a composite simulator $\mathcal{S}_\mathcal{G}$ that generates exactly $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{G})$.

**Particle types.** For each non-terminal $A \in N$ with fan-out $k_A$, introduce a v2 particle type $A$ whose state vector lies in $\mathbb{R}^{k_A \cdot d}$, where $d$ is large enough to encode any string in $T^*$ up to a bounded length. (For finite-yield grammars, $d = O(\log_2 |T| \cdot L_{\max})$ suffices, where $L_{\max}$ is the maximum yield length per non-terminal. For infinite-yield grammars, $d$ is taken to scale logarithmically with the input being parsed; the bound remains uniform per derivation step.)

**Operator algebra.** For each rule

$$
p: A(\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_{k_A}) \to f[B_1(\vec y_1), \ldots, B_r(\vec y_r)] \qquad(3.1)
$$

introduce a v3 operator $\hat f_p \in G$ of arity $r$ acting on $\mathbb{R}^{k_{B_1} d} \oplus \cdots \oplus \mathbb{R}^{k_{B_r} d} \to \mathbb{R}^{k_A d}$. The operator implements the LCFRS function $f$: it copies the appropriate $y_{j,k}$ fragments into the $\alpha_i$ slots of the output state, performing only concatenation and permutation. Since $r \le R$ and $k_A, k_{B_j} \le K$, the operator has bounded arity and bounded fragment dimension. The set of operators $\{\hat f_p : p \in P\}$ is finite (since $P$ is finite); the Lie group $G$ generated by them is finite-dimensional and finite-rank.

**Particle dynamics.** The v0 dynamics is set so that particles representing distinct non-terminals occupy disjoint regions of semantic space (achievable by giving each $A \in N$ its own $\xi_A$ anchor and a sufficiently strong well around it). The simulator's "active particle set" at any time $t$ is identified with the partial derivation tree at that point.

**Termination at terminals.** A rule $A(\sigma) \to \varepsilon$ where $\sigma \in T^*$ is realised as an atomic v2-creation event that emits the terminal string $\sigma$ as a "frozen" particle — a particle with no further v3-operator activity, contributing $\sigma$ to the yield.

**Salience and v1.5.** Once a particle has been consumed by an operator $\hat f_p$ (i.e., its state has been read into the output state of $\hat f_p$'s LHS particle), its salience $\sigma^{(i)}$ decays to zero and the particle is removed from the active set. This implements LCFRS's *linearity constraint* — each $y_{j,k}$ is read exactly once.

**Yield.** The yield of $\mathcal{S}_\mathcal{G}$ on a derivation tree is read off as the concatenation of frozen particles in left-to-right order, where order is determined by the order of v2-creation events on the start-symbol particle's reduction.

By construction, every derivation in $\mathcal{G}$ corresponds to a sequence of v2-creation and v3-operator events in $\mathcal{S}_\mathcal{G}$, and vice versa. The terminal yields coincide.

### 3.2 Every bounded composite simulator is a bounded LCFRS (soundness)

Given a composite simulator $\mathcal{S}$ satisfying the three boundedness assumptions of §1, we extract an LCFRS $\mathcal{G}_\mathcal{S}$ that generates $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{S})$.

**Non-terminals.** For each particle type $A$ in $\mathcal{S}$, introduce a non-terminal $A$ in $\mathcal{G}_\mathcal{S}$ with fan-out $k_A$. The mapping respects the framework's typing.

**Rules.** For each v3 operator $\hat f$ of arity $r$ that takes particles of types $B_1, \ldots, B_r$ and produces a particle of type $A$, introduce an LCFRS rule

$$
A(\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_{k_A}) \to f[B_1(\vec y_1), \ldots, B_r(\vec y_r)] \qquad(3.2)
$$

where the $\alpha_i$ are the concatenation/permutation patterns determined by $\hat f$'s action on the input state space.

**Linearity.** The framework's per-particle state is read by exactly one v3 operator before the particle's salience drops below $\theta_{\min}$ (assumption: salient decay implements the linearity constraint, as in §3.1). This guarantees each $y_{j,k}$ appears exactly once on the LHS — which is the LCFRS linearity constraint.

**Bounded fan-out.** $k_A$ is bounded by the assumption on per-particle dimension $k_A \cdot d \le K \cdot d$.

**Bounded rank.** Operator arity $r$ is bounded by $r_{\max}$, which is the LCFRS bounded-rank constant.

**Finite rule set.** The rule set is finite because $G$ is finite-dimensional with finite rank, hence the orbit of any single particle state under $G$ is a finite-dimensional manifold parameterised by finitely many group elements; up to the equivalence relation that identifies operators with the same string-action, $G$ has only finitely many distinct rules.

The constructed $\mathcal{G}_\mathcal{S}$ is therefore a bounded LCFRS. Its language $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{G}_\mathcal{S})$ equals $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{S})$ by construction.

## 4. Verification of the four MCS criteria

The reduction of §3 immediately implies that the composite simulator inherits the four classical MCS properties. We verify them directly to give independent confidence in the assumptions.

### 4.1 Constant growth

Each v2-creation event introduces one particle, which contributes a bounded number of terminals (bounded by the maximum yield-length of any LCFRS rule's output state). Each v3-operator action consumes a bounded number of particles ($\le r_{\max}$) and produces one. Therefore the total terminal yield of a derivation grows linearly in the number of creation events, and the number of creation events grows linearly in the derivation depth — the constant-growth property of Joshi (1985).

Formally, if $\Delta L$ is the maximum yield-extension per rule, then for a derivation tree with $|T_{\mathrm{deriv}}|$ nodes, the total yield length satisfies $|\sigma| \le \Delta L \cdot |T_{\mathrm{deriv}}|$. The yield-length set is semilinear because the rule set is finite.

### 4.2 Polynomial parsing

Parsing $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{S})$ on input $\sigma$ of length $n$ reduces to LCFRS parsing on $\mathcal{G}_\mathcal{S}$, which is $O(n^{(r_{\max}+1)K})$ by Seki et al. (1991). For the natural design point $K = 2, r_{\max} = 2$ (TAG-equivalent), this is $O(n^6)$; for $K = 3, r_{\max} = 2$, it is $O(n^9)$; for $K = 2, r_{\max} = 3$, it is $O(n^8)$. All are polynomial, satisfying Joshi's second MCS property.

The simulator's *forward-mode* (generative) inference cost is also polynomial: it is bounded by the total number of v2-creation and v3-operator events times the per-step v0 EOM integration cost, both of which are polynomial in the active-particle count $N(t) = O(n)$.

### 4.3 Beyond context-free

The cross-serial language $\{a^n b^n c^n : n \ge 1\}$ is generated by the composite simulator as follows.

**Particle types.** Introduce a v2 particle type $S$ of fan-out 2: its state vector is in $\mathbb{R}^{2d}$ with components $(\sigma_b, \sigma_c)$ — the "future $b$-string" and "future $c$-string" fragments.

**Rules / operators.**

- Initial creation: $S(\varepsilon, \varepsilon)$ creates an empty $S$-particle.
- Recursive operator $\hat f_a$ (rank 1): $S(b \cdot y_1, c \cdot y_2) \to S(y_1, y_2)$, called by emitting one terminal $a$. Implementing this in operator form: the v3 operator reads the existing $S$-particle's state $(y_1, y_2)$, prepends $b$ to $y_1$, prepends $c$ to $y_2$, and emits the terminal $a$ to the output stream.
- Termination: $S(\varepsilon, \varepsilon) \to \varepsilon$ (no operator action; terminate).
- Yield-flush: at termination, emit the $b$-string $\sigma_b$ followed by the $c$-string $\sigma_c$ to the output stream.

By construction, $n$ applications of $\hat f_a$ followed by yield-flush produce the string $a^n b^n c^n$. A pure CF (i.e. v0+v2 without v3) cannot do this because it has no mechanism to *couple* the two fragments. This is the formal counterpart of the F4 falsifier of `Advancing_The_Dynamic_Simulation_Model.md` §4.5.

The same construction generalises: for any cross-serial language $\{a^n_1 a^n_2 \ldots a^n_k\}$ on $k$ symbols, use a particle of fan-out $k-1$.

### 4.4 Below context-sensitive / Turing

We argue the upper bound by contradiction: suppose $\mathcal{S}$ generates a non-MCS language. Then the corresponding $\mathcal{G}_\mathcal{S}$ is not a bounded LCFRS. By the LCFRS construction of §3.2, this implies one of:

- $k_A$ is unbounded for some particle type $A$ (violates fan-out boundedness, hence the per-particle state dimension $k_A \cdot d$ is unbounded — violates assumption 1).
- Operator arity is unbounded (violates assumption 2).
- The operator algebra admits arbitrary word-equation solving — equivalent to the simulator deciding the word problem on free groups, which is undecidable for finitely-generated free groups in general (Stallings 1991) and certainly outside MCS.

But all three are excluded by the assumptions of §1. Therefore $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{S})$ is bounded LCFRS, hence MCS, hence below context-sensitive and below Turing.

## 5. Where the framework lands by design

### 5.1 The natural MCS design point

The natural design point — at TAG-equivalent expressivity — is

$$
K = 2, \quad r_{\max} = 2, \quad G = \mathrm{SU}(N) \text{ or } \mathrm{SO}(N) \text{ for some bounded } N \qquad(5.1)
$$

with $d$ chosen large enough to encode the maximum lexical-item complexity. This yields $O(n^6)$ parsing — the same as TAGs and CCGs — and is empirically sufficient for natural-language constructions.

A more aggressive design point — at the LCFRS-of-fan-out-3 / rank-2 tier — would be $K = 3, r_{\max} = 2$, giving $O(n^9)$ parsing. This is still polynomial and would still satisfy the MCS criteria, while admitting some reduplication and copy-language constructions that fan-out 2 cannot. Whether human language requires this tier or stops at fan-out 2 is an empirical matter that the framework leaves open.

### 5.2 Why the framework does not collapse to context-free

A pure v0+v2 system (without v3) is at most context-free: each particle creation contributes a single fragment (fan-out 1), and there is no mechanism to couple multiple fragments via a non-abelian operator. This is the F2-tier of the falsifier sequence in `Advancing_The_Dynamic_Simulation_Model.md` §4.5. The v3 extension is therefore *strictly necessary* for cross-serial dependencies, and the MCS reach of the composite is *strictly above* the v0+v2 capability.

### 5.3 Why the framework does not jump to Turing-complete

The three boundedness assumptions of §1 are *the* defence against accidental Turing-completeness. Loosening any of them lifts the framework above MCS:

- **Unbounded $G$ rank** (e.g., infinite-dimensional Lie group such as the diffeomorphism group $\mathrm{Diff}(M)$) admits arbitrary word-equation composition and decides the free-group word problem.
- **Unbounded operator arity** allows simulating an arbitrary register machine via a single rule with one register per particle, lifting the framework to Turing-complete.
- **Unbounded particle state dimension** $k_A \cdot d$ allows encoding arbitrary tape contents within a single particle, again lifting to Turing-complete.

The combination of *bounded $G$ rank*, *bounded operator arity*, and *bounded fragment dimension* is therefore what pins the framework to MCS rather than higher up. The choice is design-level, not structural — exactly the point the F6 falsifier (Minsky 2-counter task) of `Advancing_The_Dynamic_Simulation_Model.md` §4.5 is designed to test.

### 5.4 What CSL would require, mechanism-by-mechanism

§5.3 argued that loosening the boundedness assumptions sends the framework directly to Turing-complete. A natural follow-up is: *what would be required to land specifically at the context-sensitive (CSL) class*, strictly between MCS and Turing? CSL is recognised by linear-bounded automata (LBA = NSPACE($n$)) and admits non-semilinear yield-length sets such as $\{a^{2^n}\}$ and $\{a^p : p \text{ prime}\}$.

The MCS upper bound of §3 turns on a single structural fact: each derivation event extends the yield by a bounded amount, so yield-length sets are semilinear. Reaching CSL therefore requires *multiplicative* yield extension — a single event emitting a state-dependent number of terminals. Translating this into the framework's three mechanisms gives a precise diagnosis of what each mechanism would have to look like, and what would be lost in the process.

**v2 (creation) — from single-particle creation to state-dependent bulk creation.** The natural Doi–Peliti form is single-creation, $|\psi\rangle \to a^\dagger_v |\psi\rangle$, with bounded fragment dimension. The CSL form would be *state-dependent multiplicity*

$$
|\psi\rangle \to (a^\dagger_v)^{m(|\psi\rangle)} |\psi\rangle \qquad(5.2)
$$

where $m(|\psi\rangle) \ge 1$ is a function of the current configuration — for example, the number of currently-active particles or the value stored in a designated counter-particle. A single v2 event then emits a state-dependent number of terminals into the yield. The linguistic analogue is a generalised cloning rule of the form *"generate one copy of structure $S$ for every existing instance of structure $T$"*. **What breaks**: the Doi–Peliti formalism is built around fixed-multiplicity reaction terms; the Hamiltonian's nonlinearity has a bounded maximum order (typically two-body, $a^\dagger a^\dagger a a$). State-dependent multiplicity does not fit that algebra; the second-quantisation apparatus and its inherited theorems (mean-field limits, Bogoliubov transformations, the BBGKY hierarchy) cease to apply.

**v1.5 (salient decay) — from monotonic forgetting to a bounded reservoir with reactivation.** The natural form is a strict semigroup contraction: salience decays monotonically, particles below threshold are removed, the active set shrinks. The CSL form would replace this with a *bounded reservoir*: the active-particle count is hard-capped at $O(n)$ (the LBA linear-space tape bound), "decayed" particles are not destroyed but frozen in a reservoir of bounded total size retaining their state information, and a v3 operator can *reactivate* a frozen particle, restoring its full state into the active set. v1.5 then becomes equivalent to LBA tape-cell semantics — particles are tape cells, the active set is the bounded tape, decay is "not currently under the head". **What breaks**: the dissipative-semigroup identity. v1.5 is currently a strict contraction in the salience direction, which is what gives Lyapunov stability, Conley-index attractors, and the discounted-MDP analogy. Reactivation is non-monotonic in salience, breaks contraction, removes the Lyapunov function, and replaces the exponentially-stable dynamical system with a finite-state controller wrapped around a bounded register file — a fundamental change of mathematical home.

**v3 (execution) — from creation-side LCFRS rules to in-place mutation with position pointers.** The natural form is the LCFRS rule schema $A(\vec\alpha) \to \hat f \cdot [B_1(\vec y_1), \ldots, B_r(\vec y_r)]$: inputs consumed (LCFRS linearity), output created, operator a bounded-arity finite-rank Lie-group action, no word equations. The CSL form would replace this with *in-place state mutation with position pointers*: the active-particle set carries a distinguished pointer (the LBA "head"), a v3 operator takes the pointer-target particle and mutates its state in place via $x_j^A \mapsto g \cdot x_j^A$ *without* consuming it, and the operator can also *move the pointer* to a neighbour particle in some defined ordering. The set of operator types remains finite (LBA finite-control), the per-step action remains bounded, but the *number of steps* is bounded only by an LBA-style polynomial in $n$. **What breaks**: the LCFRS linearity property — each particle's state is now read and rewritten arbitrarily many times, the LCFRS rule schema no longer applies, and polynomial parsing in $O(n^{(r+1)k})$ is replaced by PSPACE-complete recognition. The gauge-equivariant reading of v3 also breaks: the current Lie-group action is naturally interpreted as a gauge transformation on the per-particle fibre, with the operator algebra forming a fibre-bundle connection; in-place mutation with position pointers does not have a natural gauge-theoretic reading. v3 leaves the field-theoretic mathematical home and becomes symbolic-machine semantics.

**The fourth, external constraint.** Even with all three of the above modifications, the framework does not land at CSL — it overshoots to Turing-complete. To stop at CSL specifically, an external constraint is required:

> *Hard cap of $O(n)$ active particles for an input of length $n$.*

Without this cap, the bounded reservoir of v1.5 grows unboundedly with derivation steps and the system becomes a register machine. The cap is the LBA linear-space property; it is what *defines* CSL. In the current framework, no such cap exists or is naturally imposed — the active-particle count is governed by the dynamics (creation rate vs decay rate), not by an external linear-in-input bound.

**Summary table.**

| Mechanism  | MCS form (current)                                              | CSL form (hypothetical)                          | What breaks                                                                |
| ---------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| v2         | bounded-multiplicity creation                                   | state-dependent bulk creation                    | Doi–Peliti fixed-order reaction kinetics; second-quantisation theorems     |
| v1.5       | monotonic semigroup contraction; particles destroyed            | bounded reservoir; reactivation                  | dissipative-semigroup identity; Lyapunov stability; discounted-MDP analogy |
| v3         | LCFRS-style creation with linearity (each fragment read once)   | in-place mutation with position pointer          | LCFRS linearity; polynomial parsing; gauge-equivariant operator algebra    |
| (external) | (none required)                                                 | hard cap of $O(n)$ active particles              | the framework has no natural place to put this                             |

**The interpretation.** Each of the three mechanisms has a single direction of *natural extension*; that extension lands at MCS, not above. CSL would require pulling each mechanism out of its natural mathematical home (Fock-space second quantisation, dissipative-semigroup theory, Lie-group / gauge-theoretic operator algebra) and adding an external linear-space constraint that has no natural place in the dynamical-system formulation. The framework's MCS commitment is therefore *principled, not lazy*: the natural mathematical homes for the three mechanisms cleanly support MCS and structurally resist CSL. This is precisely why MCS is a *good* boundary to land on — it is where the math wants to put us.

The CSL diagnosis also informs the F6 falsifier of `Advancing_The_Dynamic_Simulation_Model.md` §4.5. If F6 (the Minsky 2-counter task) succeeds, the framework has accidentally acquired one or more of the CSL-form mechanisms above — most likely state-dependent bulk creation in v2, or in-place mutation in v3 from over-eager operator design. The CSL/Turing failure mode is therefore not abstract; it has a concrete diagnosis in terms of which v2/v3 design choice went wrong.

**Comparison to attention-based transformers.** It is worth noting that the dominant neural architecture in modern language modelling — the transformer — sits in a different regime entirely. Vanilla (no chain-of-thought, no external memory) transformers are characterised by uniform TC$^0$ (Merrill & Sabharwal 2023; Strobl et al. 2023), which is well *below* MCS: empirically they fail length-generalisation past CFL on the Chomsky-hierarchy benchmark of Delétang et al. (2023). Chain-of-thought lifts the formal class — polynomial CoT to **P** and linear CoT toward **NSPACE**$(n) =$ CSL (Merrill & Sabharwal 2024) — but at the cost of replacing parallel constant-depth computation with serial autoregressive compute. Memory-augmented variants (stack-augmented transformers, Neural Turing Machines, Differentiable Neural Computers) reach CFL and CSL-class tasks empirically, but only via architectural retrofits whose composite formal class is determined by *which* augmentation is used. The composite v0+v1.5+v2+v3 simulator, by contrast, lands at MCS *natively* — the structured memory of v2's Fock-space formalism and the operator algebra of v3 supply, by their natural mathematical form, exactly the apparatus that transformers acquire only via augmentation. Section 7 of `Advancing_The_Dynamic_Simulation_Model.md` develops this comparison in detail.

**Chain-of-thought is built into v2+v3 by the LCFRS derivation structure of §3.** The reduction of §3.1 makes a stronger claim available than expressivity alone. Each completeness-direction LCFRS derivation tree, when realised as a sequence of v2-creation and v3-operator events, *is structurally a chain of thought*: non-terminal v2-particles play the role of intermediate "thoughts", v3-operator events play the role of reasoning operations, and the active-particle set above the v1.5 salience threshold plays the role of the context-window of currently-readable thoughts. The mapping is tight in seven entries (Transformer-CoT element ↔ composite-simulator analogue; the table is in §7.5 of `Advancing_The_Dynamic_Simulation_Model.md`). The framework's native CoT differs structurally from transformer CoT in three respects, all in the framework's favour: it is *tree-shaped* (driven by LCFRS rule rank) rather than a linear chain; the intermediate "thoughts" are *typed* particles with operator-labelled provenance rather than bare tokens; and internal reasoning is *structurally separated* from the external yield by the construction (only terminal v2-creation events emit to the yield channel). Critically, the boundedness assumptions of §1 are step-local rather than step-count-local, so adding more reasoning steps does not lift the class above MCS — adding "more thinking" produces longer derivations within the same class, which is the empirically correct outcome for a model of human language. The framework therefore gets the *practical* advantages of chain-of-thought (interpretable serial reasoning, intermediate structured representations, the "scratchpad" pattern) *without* the *expressivity-class costs* of transformer CoT (overshooting from TC$^0$ to P or NL, with attendant loss of polynomial parsing and overshoot above the linguistically-attested class). Exposing this native CoT to downstream tools requires only a thought-trace logging interface — an expressivity-neutral instrumentation step — discussed in §7.5 and §9 of `Advancing_The_Dynamic_Simulation_Model.md`.

### 5.5 Necessity of v3: structural and architectural arguments

§5.2 stated the headline conclusion: a pure v0+v2 system without v3 is at most context-free, so v3 is strictly necessary for the MCS reach. This subsection sharpens the conclusion in three ways. It (i) makes the v0+v2 upper bound precise via the multi-type branching process correspondence, (ii) decomposes the failure of v0+v2 into three structurally distinct obstructions, each closed by v3, and (iii) addresses the natural sceptical question of whether v3 could be folded into a richer v2 without an explicit operator-algebra mechanism. A fourth subsection collects the *non-expressivity* arguments for v3 in one place — phenomena where v3 carries weight even holding the language class fixed.

#### 5.5.1 The v0+v2 upper bound: at most CFL via multi-type branching processes

The v2 mechanism, in its natural Doi–Peliti single-creation form $|\psi\rangle \to a^\dagger_v |\psi\rangle$ with bounded fragment dimension and creation rates conditioned on the local potential field, is mathematically a *multi-type continuous-time branching process* on a finite type alphabet. Each creation event is conditioned only on the parent's type and the local field configuration around the parent; the offspring distribution depends on no other particle's state.

Such processes are well-studied in mathematical biology and population dynamics, and their formal-language characterisation is unambiguous: multi-type branching processes generate exactly the *probabilistic context-free* languages (Galton–Watson with types, where the rate kernel plays the role of production-rule distribution). The yield of any v0+v2 derivation is the leaf-string of the resulting Galton–Watson tree, and the language family is therefore *exactly CFL* — no richer.

The upper bound is *tight*: v0 alone is at most regular (`Expressivity_Bounds_For_v0_Simulator.md`), and v2's creation/destruction lifts the regular ceiling to the CFL ceiling without exceeding it. The staircase therefore reads

$$
\mathcal{L}(\text{v0}) \subseteq \text{REG}
\subsetneq
\mathcal{L}(\text{v0+v2}) \subseteq \text{CFL}
\subsetneq
\mathcal{L}(\text{v0+v1.5+v2+v3}) = \text{MCS}. \qquad(5.3)
$$

The gap between the second and fourth class is what v3 closes. It is *exactly one tier of the Chomsky hierarchy*, and that tier is the linguistically relevant one (CFL → MCS) — the tier on which natural language is empirically known to sit.

#### 5.5.2 Three structurally distinct obstructions to v0+v2 alone

The MCS witnesses of `Advancing_The_Dynamic_Simulation_Model.md` §4.5 break v0+v2 in three different ways. The taxonomy is informative: v3 is not a single ad hoc fix for a single corner case; it is a single mechanism that resolves three distinct classes of failure simultaneously.

| Falsifier | Obstruction for v0+v2 alone | v3 closure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| F4: $\{a^n b^n c^n\}$ | **Yield-coupling.** Two yield strings ($b$-string, $c$-string) must be synchronised with the $a$-recursion, but local creation rates can control only one stack at a time. | The fan-out-2 operator $\hat f_a$ couples both yield strings in a single rule firing. |
| F5: $\{ww : w \in \Sigma^*\}$ | **State duplication.** One particle's state must be copied to a second particle. v2's creation operator $a^\dagger_v$ produces a new particle in a *default* state, not a copy. | The Lie-group action $\hat D : x \mapsto (x, x)$ duplicates state by group element. |
| F3: $\mathrm{Dyck}_n$ + let-binding | **State transformation.** An existing particle's identity must be rebound mid-derivation. v2 can only create or destroy. | The operator $\hat O_{1 \to 2} \in G$ rebinds the open-bracket particle's representation in place. |

The three obstructions correspond to three structurally different deficits: the inability to *couple multiple yield strings*, the inability to *duplicate state*, and the inability to *transform state*. A single mechanism — bounded-arity, finite-rank, non-commutative operator action on the Fock space — closes all three. v3 is therefore not three patches; it is one structural mechanism that addresses three distinct expressivity bottlenecks at once.

#### 5.5.3 The fold-in alternative: extending v2 to absorb v3

A natural sceptical position holds that v3 is an unnecessary proliferation of mechanisms, and that v2 could be extended to absorb its role. The cleanest extension is to replace single-creation operators $a^\dagger_v$ with *multi-creation operators* $a^\dagger_{v_1, \ldots, v_K}$ that produce a coordinated tuple of $K$ particles in a single creation event with prescribed binding relations among the $v_i$. Such an extended v2 reaches MCS without a separately named v3, provided the coordination structure satisfies the boundedness assumptions of §1.

The fold-in alternative is **mathematically valid**; it does not, however, *eliminate* the operator-algebraic structure of v3. It only **relabels** it. Three points make this precise.

- A multi-creation $a^\dagger_{v_1, \ldots, v_K}$ that produces non-trivial binding (e.g., distinct semantic relations between the $v_i$, as in predicate–argument structure) is mathematically a Lie-group element acting on the Fock space, not a freely-commuting operator on the type alphabet. The non-commutativity $a^\dagger_{v_1, v_2} \neq a^\dagger_{v_2, v_1}$ for semantically non-equivalent role assignments is the same Lie-algebra non-abelianness that v3 exposes directly.
- The boundedness assumptions of §1 — bounded fragment dimension, bounded operator arity, finite-rank Lie group — do not relax under the fold-in. The Lie group generated by the multi-creation operators must still be finite-dimensional and finite-rank, the coordination tuple length must still be bounded, and the per-particle fragment dimension must still be bounded.
- The LCFRS reduction of §3 runs through identically with multi-creation operators in place of v3 operators; the rule schema is the same, and the four MCS criteria of §4 are verified in the same way.

The choice between (i) v2 single-creation plus v3 operator-action and (ii) v2 multi-creation absorbing v3 is therefore an **exposition choice**, not a substantive simplification. We adopt (i) for three reasons:

1. **Clean separation of mathematical apparatus.** Fock space (v2) and Lie groups (v3) are two distinct, mature mathematical formalisms with non-overlapping inherited theorem-bases. Folding them conflates two mathematically distinct structures.
2. **Clean separation of empirical falsifiers.** F1 (Dyck base) is the empirical witness of v2's necessity; F4 (cross-serial) and F5 (copy) are the empirical witnesses of v3's necessity. The two-mechanism decomposition makes the falsifier–mechanism mapping one-to-one.
3. **Clean separation of linguistic phenomena.** v2 captures *entity introduction* (referent into discourse); v3 captures *predicate–argument application* and *modification*. These are linguistically distinct phenomena, and keeping them in distinct mechanisms preserves the framework's linguistic interpretability.

We pay a small cost in mechanism count (three vs. two) and gain back: separability of mathematical apparatus, separability of empirical tests, and separability of linguistic phenomena. We judge this trade favourable.

#### 5.5.4 Beyond expressivity: v3 carries linguistic load even holding the language class fixed

The argument of §5.5.1–§5.5.3 has been about formal-language reach. v3 also carries weight in three places where the language class is not the operative concern. This subsection collects the non-expressivity arguments for v3 in one place.

**Predicate–argument order.** The classical example *"John gave Mary a book"* $\neq$ *"Mary gave John a book"* has the same participant set; only the order in which *gave* acts on its arguments differs. This non-commutativity is exactly $g_1 g_2 \neq g_2 g_1$ in the Lie-group sense and is structurally v3. Modelling it without v3 would require either (i) a much larger type alphabet enumerating role-permuted particles (combinatorial explosion in $|N|$, breaking finite-rank assumption B3), or (ii) abandoning the compositional reading of meaning altogether. Neither is a tenable alternative.

**Modification and variable binding.** Composition of modifiers (*"very red book"* = very ∘ red ∘ book) and variable binding in let-statements both reduce to operator action on existing particles' states. v2 alone offers no mechanism for either; v3 offers a single mechanism for both. Anaphora resolution (binding pronouns to their antecedents) is the same machinery: an operator-valued link from one particle to another, in v3.

**Native chain-of-thought.** The native-CoT argument of §5.4 (and §7.5 of `Advancing_The_Dynamic_Simulation_Model.md`) relies critically on v3. Each *thought* in the trace is a v3 operator firing, and the LCFRS derivation tree itself **is** the chain of thought. Strip v3, and the framework's native-CoT advantage over transformer scratchpads disappears: the residual v0+v1.5+v2 system is a CFL parser whose "thoughts" are at most stack pushes and pops. This is strictly weaker than transformer chain-of-thought, which lifts to NL or P (Merrill & Sabharwal 2024). v3 is therefore the structural prerequisite for the framework's most important architectural-comparison claim against attention-based language models.

The three points above are independent of the MCS reach claim. Even in a hypothetical world in which the language class were not the operative criterion, v3 would still carry non-redundant load in the framework. The combination of expressivity-class lift (§5.5.1–§5.5.2), the single-mechanism-three-obstructions argument (§5.5.2), the fold-in robustness (§5.5.3), and these three non-expressivity contributions (§5.5.4) is what makes v3 a *genuinely necessary* mechanism, not a stylistic addition.

## 6. Open questions and where the v2/v3 EOMs need to land

This reduction is sketched at the level of the *composite-simulator language* — the set of yields producible — without yet committing to the full v2 and v3 EOMs. Two concrete open questions follow.

**Q1. The v2 EOM specification.** The Doi–Peliti formalism named in §5.2 of `Advancing_The_Dynamic_Simulation_Model.md` provides the appropriate Fock-space starting point. The unresolved design choices are: (i) the exact dimension $d$ per particle type (and whether $d$ is shared across types or per-type), (ii) the boson vs fermion choice for the (anti-)commutation relations of $a^\dagger_v$, and (iii) the precise form of the creation/annihilation reaction terms in the v0 Hamiltonian. These are the topics of the deferred milestone `docs/Semantic_Simulator_v2_EOM.md`.

**Q2. The v3 operator-rank constraint.** The reduction assumes $G$ is finite-dimensional and finite-rank. The natural choices in the framework's lineage are $\mathrm{SU}(N)$ or $\mathrm{SO}(N)$ for some bounded $N$, building on the gauge-theory analyses in `companion_notes/P-rot-6_transformer_dynamics.md` and `geometric_deep_learning/docs/Lie_Groups_for_Gauge_Theory_Tutorial.md`. The *binding* between Lie-group rank and LCFRS fan-out is what determines whether the simulator stops at TAG-equivalent expressivity (rank-2, fan-out-2) or moves up to fan-out-3 expressivity. Empirical evidence from the F4/F5 falsifiers will guide the choice.

**Q3. Empirical confirmation of the reduction.** The reduction is constructive at the language level but does not yet account for finite-precision dynamics, integrator step-size $\Delta t$, or salience-decay rate $\tau$. Whether the *implemented* simulator at finite resolution $\epsilon$ — under the standard v0 EOM with a chosen integrator — actually realises $a^n b^n c^n$ up to depth $D^\ast_{\mathrm{MCS}}$ (the MCS-class analogue of the v0 collapse depth) is the empirical question of falsifiers F4 and F5.

## 7. Summary

The composite v0+v1.5+v2+v3 dynamic-simulation system, under the three boundedness assumptions on its operator algebra (bounded particle state dimension, bounded operator arity, finite-rank Lie group), generates exactly the class of mildly context-sensitive languages — equivalently, the class of bounded LCFRS / MCFG languages. The reduction is constructive in both directions and verifies the four classical Joshi (1985) MCS criteria.

The identification is decisive in three ways:

- **Lower bound (no slack below).** The composite system reaches strictly above context-free — it generates cross-serial dependencies and reduplication languages that pure v0+v2 cannot — because the v3 operator action couples LCFRS fragment-tuples in a way creation alone cannot.
- **Upper bound (no slack above).** The composite system stops strictly below Turing-complete because the bounded operator algebra cannot solve arbitrary word equations or simulate unbounded register machines.
- **Sharp design choice.** The MCS reach is contingent on three explicit boundedness restrictions, each of which is empirically and linguistically motivated. Loosening any one lifts the framework to Turing-complete; tightening any one drops it to context-free or below. The MCS landing-point is the natural, deliberate, defensible commitment.

This places v0+v1.5+v2+v3 among Tree-Adjoining Grammars, Combinatory Categorial Grammars, and the LCFRS family — the well-understood mildly context-sensitive grammar formalisms — but with three additional features that none of them have: (i) end-to-end continuous, differentiable dynamics; (ii) inheritance of the algebraic-physics apparatus of dissipative semigroups, second quantisation, and non-abelian gauge theory; and (iii) a force-and-flow semantic-space substrate that lifts the formalism out of pure symbolic combinatorics into a calibratable dynamical system. To our knowledge, this is the first explicit reduction of a continuous-state simulator to a mildly context-sensitive grammar formalism.
